Visual Pick and Place

A visuomotor Franka Panda policy that sorts coloured cubes into matching bins using only RGB cameras and proprioception. Trained with DAgger (Dataset Aggregation) in Isaac Lab to correct covariate shift, with an IK-based expert oracle providing labels during rollouts.

Task

A tabletop scene contains a Franka Panda arm, two coloured cubes (blue and red) with randomized poses, and two colour-matched sorting bins in disjoint workspace regions. Two 256×256 RGB cameras provide observations: a fixed table camera for global layout and a wrist camera for grasp alignment.

The student policy must place each cube in its matching bin using only cameras and 18D proprioception. During training, an expert oracle accesses privileged ground-truth object and bin poses — information unavailable at deployment.

The Covariate Shift Problem

Behavioural cloning on expert demonstrations alone suffers from distribution shift. The student executes its own imperfect actions, drifts into states the expert never visited, and small errors compound. DAgger (Dataset Aggregation, Ross et al. 2011) addresses this iteratively by collecting data on the student's actual trajectory while labeling every state with the expert's corrective action.

DAgger Algorithm

At iteration i, the algorithm:

  1. Rolls out the current student policy in the environment with β-mixed execution.
  2. Queries the expert oracle for the correct action at every visited state, regardless of who executed the previous action.
  3. Appends all (observation, expert_action) pairs to a monotonically growing aggregate dataset.
  4. Updates the student with a fixed number of supervised gradient steps on the full aggregate.
  5. Saves a new checkpoint and repeats with a lower β.

β-mixed rollouts

At each timestep, with probability β the environment executes the expert action; otherwise it executes the student action:

rollout_action = expert_action   if random() < β
                   = student_action   otherwise

The stored training label is always the expert action queried from privileged state. This is the core DAgger insight: learn what the expert would do in states the student actually visits.

β is capped at 0.9 on iteration 1 so the first round is not pure-expert (which would only duplicate the seed dataset). Two schedules are supported:

  • Linear (used in the 30-iteration run): β = 1 − (i − 1) / N, decreasing from 0.9 to ~0.03 over 30 iterations.
  • Inverse square root: β = 1 / √i — a slower decay that keeps more expert assistance in later rounds.

Expert oracle

The registered solver ik_pick_place is a privileged, model-based controller — not a learned policy. It:

  • Parses ground-truth object and bin poses into colour-matched pick-place pairs.
  • Runs a finite-state machine: APPROACH_PICK → DESCEND_PICK → GRASP → LIFT → APPROACH_PLACE → DESCEND_PLACE → RELEASE → LIFT_AFTER.
  • Tracks TCP waypoints with differential IK (DLS) at 0.75 m/s.
  • Commands a binary gripper with dwell timers (15 steps at grasp, 25 at release).

During DAgger rollouts, pick waypoints are refreshed every pre-grasp step so expert labels track cubes the student may have nudged before grasping.

Dataset aggregation

The aggregate HDF5 starts as a copy of the seed expert dataset (ik_expert_demos_vis_robomimic.hdf5). Each DAgger episode is appended in Robomimic format with student observations (obs, table_cam, wrist_cam) and expert actions as labels. The dataset grows monotonically — in the completed 30-iteration run it expanded from 85 to 685 demonstrations.

Implementation

The DAgger loop lives in scripts/train_dagger.py, with rollout collection in dagger_collector.py, HDF5 management in dagger_dataset.py, and per-iteration BC training in dagger_trainer.py. Rollouts abort early if the expert makes no progress for 500 steps, preventing the aggregate from filling with low-value stuck trajectories.

Neural Network Architecture

The student is a Robomimic BC-RNN visuomotor policy configured in bc_rnn_image_il.json. The full data flow:

table_cam (256²) ──► ResNet18 ──► SpatialSoftmax(16 kp) ──► 32D
wrist_cam (256²) ──► ResNet18 ──► SpatialSoftmax(16 kp) ──► 32D
proprio (18D) ──► passthrough
                   └─ concat → 82D → LSTM(400) → Linear(400→8)

Visual encoders (×2, separate weights)

  • Backbone: ResNet18Conv — standard ResNet-18 with 4 residual stages (2 blocks each, 8 blocks total). Trained from scratch (pretrained: false) because Isaac Sim renders differ substantially from ImageNet.
  • Input: random 224×224 crop from 256×256 during training (CropRandomizer); CHW float [0, 1] at inference.
  • Pooling: SpatialSoftmax with 16 keypoints — maps spatial feature maps to 16 (x, y) coordinates, producing a compact 32D vector per camera that preserves spatial grounding without flattening the entire feature map.
  • Why two cameras? The table camera captures bin and object layout globally; the wrist camera provides close-range grasp alignment. Each has its own encoder so the network learns camera-specific features.

Proprioception (18D)

Concatenated relative joint positions and velocities for the 7 arm joints plus 2 gripper joints. Proprioception is passed through without an encoder — it gives short-horizon dynamics (velocity, gripper state) that cameras alone cannot provide at 60 Hz control.

LSTM recurrence

  • Type: LSTM, unidirectional
  • Layers: 1
  • Hidden dimension: 400
  • Sequence length: 10 timesteps
  • Input: 82D fused vector per timestep (32 + 32 + 18)
  • Mode: closed-loop (observations fed each step, not open-loop)

Manipulation is temporally extended — grasp timing, gripper state, and motion history matter. A 10-step LSTM context captures this without the compute cost of a Transformer. One layer keeps the model small enough for real-time inference during Isaac Lab rollouts.

Policy head

actor_layer_dims: [] in the Robomimic config means no hidden MLP layers — a single linear map from the 400D LSTM hidden state directly to the 8D action output. This keeps the policy lightweight; the representational capacity sits in the ResNet encoders and LSTM rather than a deep action head.

Action space (8D): 7 relative arm joint position deltas + 1 binary gripper command. Relative actions match the expert demonstration format and generalize better across joint configurations than absolute joint targets.

Layer count summary

  • 2 × ResNet18 visual backbones (independent weights)
  • 2 × SpatialSoftmax pooling layers (16 keypoints each)
  • 1 × LSTM layer (400 hidden units)
  • 1 × linear output layer (400 → 8)
  • No additional hidden MLP between LSTM and output

Training Process

Phase 1 — Expert seed demonstrations

Before DAgger, visuomotor expert trajectories are collected with the IK solver. Only successful episodes (both cubes in matching bins) are saved. Observations include proprioception, table_cam, and wrist_cam — the same keys the student uses at deployment. The HDF5 is restructured into Robomimic format to produce the seed dataset.

Phase 2 — Behavioural cloning warm-start

DAgger requires an initial policy checkpoint capable of producing coherent (if imperfect) rollouts. A BC-RNN policy is trained on the seed dataset via Robomimic's standard training script. This warm-start checkpoint becomes the student for DAgger iteration 1.

Phase 3 — DAgger iterations

Each iteration performs three steps:

  1. Collect rollouts — 20 episodes (default) with the current checkpoint, β-mixed execution, expert relabeling on every step. Max horizon 2500 steps (~42 s at 60 Hz control).
  2. Append to aggregate — labeled episodes written to the growing HDF5. Failures are included; expert labels remain valid corrections even on unsuccessful trajectories.
  3. Train — 5000 BC gradient steps on the entire aggregate dataset. Weights warm-start from the previous iteration's checkpoint. New checkpoint saved as dagger_iter_N.pth.

Hyperparameters

  • DAgger iterations: 30 (completed run)
  • Episodes per iteration: 20
  • Gradient steps per iteration: 5000
  • Rollout horizon: 2500 steps
  • β schedule: linear, β_start = 0.9
  • Loss: L2 on actions (deterministic regression; expert is near-deterministic)
  • Optimizer: Adam, learning rate 1×10⁻⁴
  • Batch size: 32 (config default)
  • Sequence length: 10 (aligned with LSTM horizon)
  • Control rate: 60 Hz (120 Hz sim, decimation 2)
  • Episode timeout: 45 s

Data loading

Proprioception is cached in memory (hdf5_cache_mode: low_dim) while RGB images are streamed from disk. This keeps RAM usage manageable as the aggregate dataset grows with image data across iterations. Random 224×224 crops from 256×256 images provide mild augmentation during training.

Training results (30-iteration run)

  • Aggregate dataset grew from 85 → 685 demonstrations (+20 per iteration).
  • L2 training loss decreased from 0.0045 (iter 1) to 0.0014 (iter 7), then rose as more on-policy failure data accumulated at low β.
  • Rollout success rate stayed at 18–20 / 20 while β ≥ 0.5, then dropped as student execution dominated (0 / 20 at iterations 24–30 when β ≈ 0.03).
  • Each 5000-step training phase took ~10 s on GPU.

The late-iteration success drop reflects the intentional shift toward fully on-policy rollouts: the student visits harder states without expert intervention, providing corrective labels for those regions in the aggregate even when episodes fail.
