IIT Madras · European Hyperloop Week 2024

Avishkar Hyperloop

Work on IIT Madras's Hyperloop pod across two roles — first as an aerodynamics team member studying tube-configuration drag through CFD, then as Mechanical Lead coordinating the team and authoring technical research on shock-wave behaviour in Hyperloop systems. The pod represented IIT Madras and India at European Hyperloop Week 2024 in Zurich, securing 2nd place in the research category.

Aerodynamics Team Member

Mar 2023 – Aug 2023

Hyperloop pods operate inside partially evacuated tubes, and the geometry of that tube strongly influences aerodynamic drag — often the dominant energy cost at transonic speeds. A conventional single-tube layout leaves the pod surrounded by a long column of air that must be displaced and accelerated as the vehicle moves. We investigated whether a connected tube configuration, where the pod travels through a tube that is vented or coupled differently along its length, could reduce that penalty.

I ran dynamic CFD simulations in Ansys Fluent across multiple pod–tube configurations, varying flow conditions to compare drag on the same pod geometry in a standard tube versus the connected-tube arrangement. At 0.4 Mach, the connected configuration showed a 56% reduction in drag relative to the single-tube baseline — a substantial improvement for a system where even modest drag savings translate directly into lower propulsion power and operating cost.

The results were strong enough to pursue intellectual property protection: a patent has been filed on the connected tube configuration as a novel approach for Hyperloop systems. The simulation below illustrates the flow behaviour studied during this work.

Mechanical Lead

Aug 2023 – Jul 2024

As Mechanical Lead, I was responsible for the human and operational side of building the pod as much as the engineering itself — recruiting new team members, coordinating procurement of materials and components, and keeping the mechanical workstream on schedule ahead of competition deadlines. Alongside that management role, I continued to contribute technically to the pod's aerodynamic and structural story.

A central piece of my research was an analysis of shock waves in Hyperloop systems, documented in the team's European Hyperloop Week 2024 final research submission. When a pod accelerates inside a near-vacuum tube, choked flow can produce leading and trailing shocks. Shock–boundary-layer interaction (SBLI) then separates the boundary layer and forms vortices that increase drag and couple with low-frequency shock oscillations — affecting stability and comfort. Transient CFD in Ansys Fluent tracked lift, drag, and moment through the unsteady acceleration phase into a steadier regime. The report also surveyed mitigation strategies such as micro vortex generators, porous surfaces to weaken shock reflections, and cross passages for pressure equalization. I authored the technical report on this topic as part of the competition submission.

That report was presented at European Hyperloop Week 2024 in Zurich, Switzerland, where Avishkar Hyperloop placed 2nd in the research category, representing IIT Madras and India on an international stage.

On the structural side, the team engineered the pod's chassis and aerodynamic shell with Ansys Mechanical, targeting deformations below 0.5 mm under load so the outer shape — and therefore the aerodynamic performance we had worked hard to optimize — would hold in real operating conditions.
